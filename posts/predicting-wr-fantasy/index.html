I’ll be very up front with you – I’m a big sports fan. One of the things I’ve never done, though, is try to play fantasy football. I love to watch football, and I’m scared that playing fantasy would stress me out and make me enjoy the game less because I’d be too worried about how my players are doing. So, I’ve just never done it. Nonetheless, the point of this post is that I used machine learning to try and predict which wide receivers would break out this year in fantasy football.

Code 
library(tidyverse)
library(tidymodels)
library(bonsai)
library(cfbfastR)
library(gt)

set.seed(1234)

wrstats <- read_csv("https://mattwaite.github.io/sportsdatafiles/wrdraftedstatswithratings.csv") %>% mutate(FantPt = case_when(FantPt == 0 ~ NA, TRUE ~ FantPt))

currentwrstats <- read_csv("https://mattwaite.github.io/sportsdatafiles/currentwrstats.csv")

wrselected <- wrstats %>% 
  select(
    name,
    year,
    college_team,
    nfl_team,
    overall,
    total_yards,
    total_touchdowns,
    FantPt,
    #total_ypr,
    rating
  ) %>% na.omit()

player_split <- initial_split(wrselected, prop = .8)

player_train <- training(player_split)
player_test <- testing(player_split)

player_recipe <- 
  recipe(FantPt ~ ., data = player_train) %>%
  update_role(name, year, college_team, nfl_team, new_role = "ID")

#summary(player_recipe)

xg_mod <- boost_tree(
  trees = tune(), 
  learn_rate = tune(),
  tree_depth = tune(), 
  min_n = tune(),
  loss_reduction = tune(), 
  sample_size = tune(), 
  mtry = tune(), 
  ) %>% 
  set_mode("regression") %>% 
  set_engine("xgboost")

lightgbm_mod <- 
  boost_tree() %>%
  set_engine("lightgbm") %>%
  set_mode(mode = "regression")

xg_workflow <- 
  workflow() %>% 
  add_model(xg_mod) %>% 
  add_recipe(player_recipe)

lightgbm_workflow <- 
  workflow() %>% 
  add_model(lightgbm_mod) %>% 
  add_recipe(player_recipe)

xgb_grid <- grid_latin_hypercube(
  trees(),
  tree_depth(),
  min_n(),
  loss_reduction(),
  sample_size = sample_prop(),
  finalize(mtry(), player_train),
  learn_rate()
)

player_folds <- vfold_cv(player_train)

xgb_res <- tune_grid(
  xg_workflow,
  resamples = player_folds,
  grid = xgb_grid,
  control = control_grid(save_pred = TRUE)
)

best_rmse <- select_best(xgb_res, "rmse")

final_xgb <- finalize_workflow(
  xg_workflow,
  best_rmse
)

xg_fit <- 
  final_xgb %>% 
  fit(data = player_train)

lightgbm_fit <- 
  lightgbm_workflow %>% 
  fit(data = player_train)

xgpredict <- 
  xg_fit %>% 
  predict(new_data = player_train) %>%
  bind_cols(player_train) 

lightgbmpredict <- 
  lightgbm_fit %>% 
  predict(new_data = player_train) %>%
  bind_cols(player_train) 

#metrics(xgpredict, FantPt, .pred)

#metrics(lightgbmpredict, FantPt, .pred)

xgtestpredict <- 
  xg_fit %>% 
  predict(new_data = player_test) %>%
  bind_cols(player_test) 

lightgbmtestpredict <- 
  lightgbm_fit %>% 
  predict(new_data = player_test) %>%
  bind_cols(player_test) 

#metrics(xgtestpredict, FantPt, .pred)

When making my model, I accounted for their overall pick in the draft, their total yards and touchdowns, and their draft rating. In my original model, I had things like offensive explosiveness and their college team’s offensive success, but unfortunately, there were some data issues and I was very limited in terms of the predictors I had. I used Light GBM and got my adjusted r squared value to about 49%.

First off, we have Jaxon Smith-Njigba, who hails from Ohio State (ew) and is headed to the Seattle Seahawks. I predict that he’ll score about 116 fantasy points, which is about on the level of Allen Lazard and Chris Godwin. He had 1,606 yards in his big season in 2021-22.

I predicted that Quentin Johnston of TCU would be right behind Smith-Njigba at 115 points. He had 2,190 yards over his three years with the Horned Frogs, including 1,069 last season. He could be a legitimate asset for Justin Herbert and the Chargers in his first year.

Jordan Addison, the 23rd pick of the draft, might be a legitimate threat for the Vikings in his first year. In two seasons at Pitt and one at USC, he garnered 3,134 yards and 29 TDs. It’s not arguable that he’s had a consistently good college career.

Perhaps the surprise of my picks comes in Jalin Hyatt, a former Tennessee Volunteer. He was the 73rd pick in the draft, but might make a splash in the league with the Jets and Aaron Rodgers. Rodgers will certainly be looking for targets in New York, and Hyatt just might be one.

The last of my top five is Jayden Reed of Michigan State. This one will be interesting to watch, since he’s going to a Green Bay team that is now led by the likes of Jordan Love. Reed grabbed 2,069 yards and 18 TDs with the Spartans.
